An Idea

The process of creating these chess pieces was labor-intensive but spiritually fulfilling. I poured resin into molds, each piece requiring patience and precision. As I worked, I thought of the symbolism the bishops would hold. The number 106 came from the exact number of weeks I would spend on my mission, and it gave me a sense of purpose and focus as I shaped the pieces. I envisioned how these bishops would be my companions, each one standing for a week of challenges, growth, and learning. These pieces weren’t just chess figures; they became representations of faith, resilience, and dedication.

Still Just An Idea

Once I had completed all 106 bishops, I felt a sense of accomplishment and excitement for my mission ahead. I decided that each week, I would set out a bishop in a different place that reminded me of the experiences, feelings, or lessons I encountered during that week. Whether I was in a bustling city, a quiet village, or a remote area, I would carefully place one of the bishops in a spot that had significance to the events or emotions I was experiencing. It could be near a sunset, by a landmark, or on a quiet street corner—each spot would hold its own story.



Alright Now We're Getting Somewhere

I took a picture of the bishop wherever I placed it, creating a visual journal of sorts. Each picture was a snapshot of the week, a way to preserve the memory, and a way to reflect on how far I had come. I was always intentional about the locations, finding places that held meaning and reminded me of the lessons I was learning in the mission field. These pictures weren’t just about capturing the environment; they were about capturing the growth I was undergoing spiritually and personally. The bishop chess pieces, once static in my hands, became dynamic markers of my mission's journey.

Overelaborating On a Basic Idea
As the weeks passed, I looked forward to setting out my bishop for the week. It became a ritual, a quiet moment of reflection amid the busyness of missionary work. Each time I placed a bishop, I would pause and think about the experiences that week had brought. I would reflect on the people I met, the challenges I faced, and the progress I had made in my own faith. It became a grounding moment in the midst of all the change and growth I was undergoing. The bishops, once crafted and placed, would silently watch over me as I navigated the next chapter of my life.

Nearing The End Of The Mission
At the end of my two-year mission, I had a collection of 106 photographs, each one telling the story of a week. I also had all the bishops, now worn and weathered by time, each one representing a specific part of my journey. The collection wasn’t just a physical reminder of where I had been; it was a spiritual journal that chronicled the highs and lows of my mission. Each bishop had a story, a place in the world, and a lesson that helped shape the person I had become.
Looking Back
Looking back now, those 106 resin bishops are not just symbols of a game, but of a journey—a two-year pilgrimage that shaped my character and deepened my faith. Every time I look at them, I am reminded of the lessons learned, the people I met, and the memories I created. The bishops I placed around the world became more than just pieces of resin; they became silent witnesses to my transformation, each one carrying a week’s worth of experiences, growth, and a reminder of the importance of perspective in life, just like the bishop in the game of chess.
